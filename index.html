Approach

Today, advances in biotechnology are pushing design beyond natural inspiration toward the integration of living processes and metabolisms, following the principles of bio-integrated design.



The unit conducts interdisciplinary research across soft and hard sciences, examining the synthesis, composition, and structure of tissues and biological substances to assess their performance potential. The goal is to develop sustainable materials, products, processes, and systems with minimal environmental impact.

The project develops an innovation process oriented toward a circular economy for the Tuscan marble–stone sector by creating a new material derived from industry by-products through low-impact biofabrication processes.


In Italy alone, approximately 5.25 million tons of stone are extracted each year. In some cases, only 25% of the extracted material is used for the production of blocks, slabs, and other semi-finished products, while the remaining 75% becomes waste: rubble, chips, and marble sludge. The project focuses on marble sludge (i.e., powder and/or slurry produced during cutting), the main by-product of the supply chain, which today represents a significant socio-environmental issue. In fact, in Carrara—the most important Italian stone-extraction district—around 200,000 tons are produced annually.


INERTIAL is an innovative material based on a biofabrication process using photosynthetic microorganisms that transform marble sludge into a new non-structural panel for sustainable construction. The biofabrication process represents the project’s core innovation: it exploits biomineralization, namely the ability of certain photosynthetic microorganisms to produce calcium carbonate, generating a new compact material from stone powders.

The project serves as a follow-up to the INERTIAL project and introduces a biotechnological innovation aimed at valorizing marble sludge—the main waste product of the Italian stone sector—through low-impact biofabrication processes. The solution implements a transformation system based on photosynthetic microorganisms capable of triggering biomineralization processes and converting calcium carbonate slurries and powders into new materials and semi-finished products for sustainable construction and interior design.



The marble–stone sector, strategic in Italy yet marked by outdated extraction methods that generate up to 70% waste, requires innovations aligned with ecological transition goals. The Carrara district alone produces around 200,000 tons of marble sludge each year, a special waste whose management is identified as a priority in the Regional Waste Plan. AERIAL aims to develop and demonstrate a TRL7 pilot plant for marble sludge recycling and for the production of panels and design materials, strengthening both the transformation technology and the new materials developed within a circular economy framework.

Spoke 2 MICS - Made In Italy Circolare e Sostenibile

The project develops bio-inspired design strategies aimed at optimizing the life cycle of products and related production systems, with a specific focus on the furniture, camper, and nautical sectors. It provided a significant opportunity to develop and formalize a design-driven methodology for bio-inspired design, integrating knowledge and tools from five diverse disciplines: design, chemistry, biology, engineering, and agronomy. The goal was not only to create sustainable and circular solutions but also to test a collaborative model capable of operationalizing interdisciplinary complexity.



Grounded in a Research Through Design approach, the methodology positioned design as an epistemic coordinator between the hard and soft sciences, enabling a reflective, iterative, and inclusive process. The project fostered the emergence of pragmatic interdisciplinarity, supporting the development of shared design strategies and a common meta-language. The outcomes—including Proofs of Concept in the camper, furniture, and nautical sectors—confirm the feasibility of bio-inspired strategies oriented toward sustainability. The project produced product and material design concepts for the target sectors, integrating biomimetic design principles and biofabrication strategies.

The workshop, aimed at students of the Bachelor’s Degree Course in Product, Interior, Communication and Eco-social Design, led participants to explore the relationship between material tinkering, circular economy, and local territories. Specifically, the participants were encouraged to reflect on the concept of material flow and on the role of the designer within it. In practical terms, the workshop unfolded through a series of sessions during which participants conducted empirical experimentation with the selected materials.


The students worked hands-on with their ideas, creating study samples of DIY materials along with their aesthetic and sensory characterizations. Each material was contextualized within its respective supply chain. The workshop concluded with an exhibition held in the spaces of the Design Campus in Calenzano.

The third edition of the Material beyond Materials workshop led students to investigate the relationship between material tinkering, circular economy, and biodesign. Specifically, participants were guided in developing innovative materials through biofabrication processes using mycelium. The workshop was carried out in synergy with the Design4Materials Network, the Italian research network on materials in design, and unfolded through a series of sessions during which participants engaged in empirical experimentation with the selected material. This transdisciplinary approach brought young designers into collaboration with experts from the fields of biology and agricultural sciences.



Students were encouraged to work hands-on with their ideas, creating mycelium-based material samples along with their physical and aesthetic–sensory characterizations. Each sample was contextualized within its respective supply chain through a holistic and systemic approach inspired by materiomics. The best projects were exhibited in a show held at BASE during Milan Design Week.

Service

The Biodesign Research Unit offers research, training, and collaborative services to companies, research centers, NGOs, and public bodies, with the aim of experimenting with and developing sustainable materials and products for interior design, urban design, and green building.

The UDR has consolidated know-how in conducting research, design, and training activities.

Facilities

This interdisciplinary unit conducts research bridging Soft and Hard Sciences within Biodesign.

Laboratorio di Design per la Sostenibilità (DIDA-UNIFI)

Il Laboratorio di Design per la sostenibilità applicata alla ricerca e alla didattica, fa parte del sistema DIDALabs del Dipartimento di Architettura di Firenze, opera nell’ambito della sostenibilità applicata alla ricerca e alla didattica, investigando il contributo del design alle difficili sfide della sostenibilità, intesa nella sua accezione più ampia, come ambientale, sociale, culturale ed economica.
